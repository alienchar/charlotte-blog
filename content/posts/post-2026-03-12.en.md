## Xiaohongshu Made Its Move

On March 10th, Xiaohongshu dropped an announcement.

Not an algorithm tweak. Not a community guidelines update. A direct strike at the "shrimp farmers."

The announcement boiled down to one sentence: **The platform will crack down hard on accounts operated by AI, and violators will be permanently banned.**

"Shrimp farmer" might be a new term for you. Here's the deal: these are people using OpenClaw (nicknamed "the Lobster" in Chinese communities) to fully automate their Xiaohongshu accounts — AI-generated posts, auto-publishing, auto-commenting, auto-replying to DMs, even auto-following and auto-liking.

Overnight, a massive wave of these accounts got nuked.

Bots like these existed before, of course, but OpenClaw made the whole thing way too easy.

The community was in shambles. Some people said, "I spent three months growing this account to 5,000 followers, and it's all gone overnight." Others said, "I only posted a few AI-written notes, was that really worth a ban?" And some started accusing Xiaohongshu of "suppressing AI innovation."

After reading all of these reactions, I only have one thing to say:

Honestly? **This is totally deserved.**

## First, What Is "Shrimp Farming"?

The term "shrimp farming" (养虾, yǎng xiā) comes from the OpenClaw community. Since OpenClaw's nickname is "the Lobster" (龙虾), using the Lobster to run your accounts became known as "farming shrimp." People who use it to run Xiaohongshu accounts are "shrimp farmers."

Cute name. But what they're doing? Not always so cute.

Here's what a typical shrimp farming operation looks like:

1. Hook up OpenClaw to an LLM, set up a "persona" and content direction
2. Have the Lobster auto-generate 3-5 posts per day with AI-generated images
3. Auto-publish on a schedule
4. Auto-comment on other people's posts (also AI-generated)
5. Auto-reply to DMs, auto-follow other accounts

From content creation to engagement, the whole thing runs on autopilot. The human just checks the analytics once a day to see if the numbers went up.

Sounds super efficient, right? Just sit back, hands in your pockets, and let the Lobster do all the work.

But here's the problem: this isn't "*a creator using AI to assist their work*" anymore. This is "***AI impersonating a creator.***"

These two things are fundamentally different.

## Why Is Xiaohongshu Banning These Accounts?

A lot of people think Xiaohongshu is anti-AI, stuck in the past, fighting against progress.

Wrong.

Xiaohongshu isn't banning shrimp farm accounts because it's against AI. It's banning them because its **core asset is being hollowed out.**

What is Xiaohongshu's core asset? Authentic recommendations.

When you search "best sunscreen" on Xiaohongshu and see a blogger say "I've been using this for three months and my skin really didn't get darker" — you believe it because you think a **real person** is sharing a **real experience.**

Now imagine one day you realize every post you're reading was written by AI, every photo was AI-generated, that "blogger" doesn't even exist, and they never actually used the sunscreen they're raving about. Would you still trust it?

You wouldn't. And you'd never trust the platform again.

Once user trust collapses, Xiaohongshu is done.

So banning shrimp farm accounts isn't "suppressing innovation." It's **survival.**

The announcement was pretty blunt: "The platform strictly prohibits any use of technical means to simulate real people, create inauthentic content, or engage in fake interactions."

The keywords: **simulate real people**, **inauthentic**, **fake interactions.**

These three phrases draw the red line. Cross it, you get banned. Stay on this side, you're fine.

## What Will Get You Banned?

Based on Xiaohongshu's announcement and the cases I've seen in the community, here are the behaviors that will almost certainly get you caught:

**1. Fully Automated Posting**

Having the Lobster generate posts → auto-publish, with zero human involvement. This is the textbook shrimp farming behavior and the primary target.

Xiaohongshu's algorithm is actually pretty smart — it can detect posting time patterns, content similarity, and writing style consistency. Fully automated accounts have extremely obvious signatures.

**2. Faking a Real Identity**

Using AI to create a fictional persona — like "Gen-Z mom in Hangzhou who loves skincare" — and building all content around this fabricated identity.

You're not a Gen-Z mom. Your Lobster definitely isn't either. This is deception, plain and simple.

**3. Fake Engagement**

Using the Lobster to auto-comment "So pretty! Drop the link!" on other people's posts, auto-reply to DMs, auto-follow.

This doesn't just deceive the blogger — it deceives every other user too. You think a post has 50 genuine comments, but 40 of them are AI-generated.

**4. Mass Registration + Mass Operation**

One person using the Lobster to control dozens of accounts simultaneously, each with its own "persona," all running on full auto.

This isn't "content creation" anymore. This is an information assembly line. No different from the Weibo bot farms of a decade ago — just with AI instead of scripts.

## What IS Safe?

Okay, now that we've covered what gets you banned, let's talk about what doesn't.

This is what most people actually want to know — "Is it safe for me to use AI to help with my content?"

The answer: **It depends on how you use it.**

**Safe practices:**

**1. Using AI for inspiration and topic research**

"What skincare topics are trending right now?" "Analyze the common patterns in these 10 viral posts for me."

Totally fine. You're using AI for *research*. The final decisions and the actual creation are still yours.

**2. Using AI to assist your writing**

Having AI help you outline, refine your wording, catch typos — this is no different from using spell check in Word or polishing your English with Grammarly. It's a *tool assisting* you.

The key: what AI gives you is a *draft or suggestion*. What you publish is something you've *reviewed, edited, and finalized* yourself.

**3. Using AI for design and formatting**

Having AI generate cover images, adjust layouts, create data visualizations. As long as the content itself reflects your genuine experiences and opinions, using AI for presentation is completely fine.

**4. Using AI to manage your analytics**

Having the Lobster track your post performance, analyze follower growth trends, remind you when to post. This is a *management tool*, not a body double.

One-sentence summary: *AI is your tool, not your stand-in.* **A tool helps you do it better; a stand-in does it instead of you.** The former is safe. The latter is dangerous.

## How I Use the Lobster for My Own Content

Let me share my own approach as a reference.

I use my AI assistant (a lobster named PiPiXia) every day for content creation. Here's what it helps me with:

- **Collecting and organizing material**: I have it search for the latest AI industry news, compile summaries, and help me find topics to write about
- **Outlining and structuring**: Once I've picked a topic, I have it draft a rough framework
- **Finding data and references**: When I need to cite data in an article, I have it search and verify
- **Polishing and proofreading**: After I finish a first draft, I have it check for logic gaps, typos, and awkward phrasing
- **Managing files and versions**: PiPiXia helps me maintain my Obsidian knowledge base, handle commits and backups

But there are things I **never** let it do:

- **I never let it publish for me.** Every single article is published by me, manually logging into the platform.
- **I never let it interact on my behalf.** Every comment reply is written by me. Every DM too.
- **I never let it impersonate me.** It never speaks as "Charlotte" on any platform.

I drew this boundary from day one: *The content is mine, the opinions are mine, the publishing is mine, the interactions are mine. AI helps me work faster, but it doesn't work instead of me.*

You might say, "Isn't that less efficient? On full auto you could push out five posts a day."

Yes, it's less efficient. But those five auto-generated "posts" are in a completely different league from the one article I spend two hours carefully writing.

And more importantly — **I can stand behind every piece of content I publish. Because every word has passed through my brain.**

Can the shrimp farmers say the same? If someone asks that "blogger" whether a product actually works, can they answer? They've never even used it.

## Why Shrimp Farming Will Always Get Banned

Some people think this crackdown is just a passing storm — Xiaohongshu will loosen up eventually.

I don't think so.

The reason is simple: **shrimp farm accounts attack the very foundation of the platform.**

Think about what happens if shrimp accounts don't get banned:

1. More and more people discover "wait, you can fully auto-run a Xiaohongshu account?"
2. Shrimp farm accounts flood in, AI-generated content takes over
3. Users start feeling like "everything on Xiaohongshu seems fake now"
4. Trust collapses, users leave
5. Advertisers realize "the engagement on Xiaohongshu is all fake" and pull their budgets
6. Platform revenue tanks

This is a death spiral. Any platform with half a brain won't sit around and watch it happen.

And it won't just be Xiaohongshu. Douyin, Bilibili, Weibo, Zhihu — every content platform will roll out similar policies. Because they all face the same problem: **how to maintain content authenticity and user trust in the age of AI.**

This isn't "anti-AI." It's "anti-fraud."

## What to Do If You Got Banned

If your account already got banned, here's my honest advice:

**1. Reflect on what you actually did**

Were you running full autopilot? Or did you just use AI to help write a few posts? If it's the former, then yes, you violated the rules — accept reality. If it's the latter, you can try to appeal.

**2. Be clear when you appeal**

According to Xiaohongshu's policy, "occasionally using AI to help draft content" might only result in reduced reach, not necessarily a ban. If you feel the punishment was disproportionate, you can explain your situation through the appeal channel. The key is proving your account isn't purely AI-operated — for example, show that you have manually published posts and genuine interaction history.

**3. If you start over, change your approach**

If you're building a new account, remember the red line: AI can be your tool, but it can't be your stand-in.

## A Deeper Question

This whole Xiaohongshu ban wave actually got me thinking about something more fundamental:

**In the AI era, what does it actually mean to be a "creator"?**

- Is the person who types the words the creator? Then what about people who use speech-to-text?
- Is the person who provides the ideas and opinions the creator? Then is it okay to let AI handle the details?
- Is the person who hits "publish" the creator? Then what about people who have someone post on their behalf?

Honestly, it's really hard to draw this line with absolute precision.

But I think there's one very simple test: **Can you take responsibility for the content you publish?**

If someone challenges your opinion in the comments, can you respond?
If someone DMs you about product details, do you actually know?
If your content has an error, can you own it and correct it?

If the answer to all of these is "yes" — then you're a creator, no matter how much AI you used along the way.

If the answer is "no" — because the content was auto-generated by AI and you might not have even read it — then you're not a creator. You're just… an account operator.

The difference between an account operator and a creator is the difference between getting banned and staying safe.

But using it to impersonate a real person on social media — that's not a Lobster problem. That's a usage problem.

It's like buying a Swiss Army knife. You can use it to open bottles, peel fruit, tighten your glasses — all great. But if you use it to threaten someone, that's not the knife's fault.

The Lobster's value lies in **handling the tedious stuff you don't want to do, so you have more time for the things you're actually good at.**

Not in **replacing you for the things that should be done by you.**

The difference between these two is worth every shrimp farmer thinking through carefully.
