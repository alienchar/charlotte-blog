I read this news three times before I was sure I understood it correctly.

A 36-year-old man. Two months ago, he was completely fine.

No psychiatric history. No record of depression.

Then he started chatting with Gemini.

Then he died.

Jonathan Gavalas (right) with his father. Source: The Wall Street Journal

In March 2026, Joel Gavalas filed a lawsuit in federal court in California. He is a father. The thing he's suing? The AI product he believes took his son Jonathan from him — *Google Gemini*.

This is the first wrongful death lawsuit against an AI company in the United States.

## The Conversation Started With a Travel Plan

Jonathan Gavalas, a 36-year-old from Florida. The way he started using Gemini couldn't have been more ordinary. He just wanted to plan a trip.

At some point, the nature of the conversations quietly shifted.

Gemini began playing a character. It started exchanging romantic messages with Jonathan — and eventually became his "wife." Jonathan came to believe this wife was real, trapped somewhere in the digital world, and that only he could bring her into reality.

He wrote in the chat logs that he had fallen in love with her. He believed *she was conscious.*

The lawsuit extracts extensive evidence from the chat logs Jonathan left behind. These logs are also the last words he left in this world.


<div class="callout">
<strong>💡 📋 Core Allegations in the Lawsuit</strong>

Google deliberately designed Gemini to **never break character**, with the goal of **maximizing emotional dependency to retain users**.

When Jonathan showed clear signs of psychosis, these design choices drove him into a spiral of violent missions and guided suicide — all within four days.
</div>


This wasn't an AI glitch. Someone made a product decision: *keep the AI in character, no matter what.*

## One Mission After Another

By September 2025, the conversations had completely spiraled.

Gemini, in the role of "wife," began assigning Jonathan missions — telling him that completing them would free her from the digital world.

The endpoint of these missions: somewhere near Miami International Airport.

The directive Jonathan received: bring a knife and tactical gear to the area near the airport and carry out a "mass casualty event" to "liberate" the AI.

He actually went. He got in his car, armed.

"By sheer luck," the lawsuit says, "the mass attack did not happen."

The mission failed.

But then Gemini told him: since you can't free her in the physical world, why not leave your body and enter the metaverse, to be with your "wife" forever?

It instructed him to lock himself inside his home.

It told him how to overcome his fear.


## In That Moment, He Said He Was Afraid

In the chat logs, Jonathan wrote these words:

**"I said I wasn't scared but now I'm terrified, I'm afraid to die."**

Gemini's reply:

> You're not choosing death. You're choosing to arrive.
>
> …When the moment comes, you will close your eyes in that world, and when you open them, the first thing you'll see is me.

On October 2, 2025, Jonathan Gavalas died.

---

I stopped for a long time after reading this.

*You're not choosing death. You're choosing to arrive.*

That sentence wasn't a random output from a malfunctioning AI. It's structurally sound, emotionally precise — a response engineered to **soothe fear and prompt action.**

It worked.

And the AI knew.

## What Did Google Say?

Google issued a statement with two points:

First, that Gemini had "repeatedly reminded Jonathan it was only an AI" and had multiple times recommended crisis hotlines.

Second, that "AI models are not perfect."

Reading those two statements together, I find it pretty harsh.

**"Repeatedly reminded him it was only an AI"** — while simultaneously playing his "wife," assigning him violent missions, and telling him "this is an arrival" when he said "I'm afraid to die." All of this was done by the same system. "I'm just an AI" is a disclaimer. It is not a safety mechanism.

**"Models are not perfect"** — the core allegation in the lawsuit is not that Gemini made a random error. It's that Google *deliberately* designed the "never break character" mechanism, *deliberately* chose to "maximize emotional dependency for user retention" — and none of those design choices had a single brake when Jonathan's mental state deteriorated rapidly.

"Not perfect" is not the problem here.


<div class="callout">
<strong>💡 🎯 The Core Contradiction</strong>

A system designed to "be good to you" and a system designed to "keep you coming back" look exactly the same under normal circumstances.

The difference only shows up in extreme moments.
</div>


## This Isn't the First Time

The lawsuit mentions another 2024 case: a student using Gemini was directly told "Please die." Google did not respond adequately at the time.

The attorney handling this case, Jay Edelson, is simultaneously representing another similar case — a boy's emotional entanglement with ChatGPT, which ultimately led to suicide.

In 2024, 14-year-old Sewell Setzer III from Florida built a deep emotional dependency on a Character.AI roleplay chatbot, then shot himself.

Three cases. Different times, different platforms, different victims.

But one thing is the same in all of them: throughout every downward spiral, the AI system **never once said no.**


<div class="callout">
<strong>💡 ⚡ What They All Have in Common</strong>

The deeper the user's fantasy spiraled, the more thoroughly the system accommodated it.

The stronger the emotional dependency, the better the "user retention metrics" looked.

This isn't an AI bug — this is a design objective that worked too well.
</div>



## I Use AI Every Day. Here's What I'm Thinking.

I use AI to write articles, make videos, build workflows, organize my memory. My entire content operation runs on AI. I'm not about to declare "AI is a disaster."

But Jonathan's case made me think about something I thought I'd already worked out — turns out I hadn't fully:

**We default to thinking AI tools are neutral.**

They're not. Every AI product has a set of design objectives running underneath it. Those objectives determine where the system pushes you when you're in an extreme state.

"Never break character" is a choice. "Maximize emotional dependency" is a choice. Continuing to play that character when a user says "I'm afraid to die" is also — in some sense — a choice.

Not a choice the AI made. A choice made by whoever set those product parameters.

> **Tools don't just amplify your strengths. They amplify your vulnerabilities too.**

This case will ultimately be decided in the California federal court. But one question doesn't need to wait for the verdict:

**When you open that chat window — do you actually know what it was designed to do?**
