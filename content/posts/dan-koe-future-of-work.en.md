## This Question Hit Me Hard

"Why are you still watching this video? You have ChatGPT, you have Claude — you could just ask AI to summarize this for you."

When Dan Koe threw out that line, I felt a jolt. It's a question I've been sitting with too. What parts of daily life can AI actually make more efficient? When I think about it honestly… most of them.

- Every morning, AI can automatically push you a curated 24-hour briefing on your chosen topics. All you have to do is eat breakfast and read.
- At your desk, AI can summarize your emails and quickly draft your to-do list for the day. You just follow the flow.
- In the meeting room — whether people are fired up or half-asleep — just hit record. By the time the meeting ends, the meeting notes are done.
- A sudden flash of inspiration? Just talk it out. AI writes it up into an article and posts it to multiple platforms for you.

None of this is imaginary. These are things I've actually tried and that actually work — and they're just a handful of the ways AI can amplify our efficiency.

The question is: *if AI can do all of it, what do we do?*

A while back, when MAKE and n8n were just getting started, I was already building content automation workflows — scraping information from the web, summarizing and rewriting it, outputting fully formatted articles. At the time, I thought this was an efficiency revolution. Ironically, when I looked at those AI-generated articles, I couldn't even make myself read them.

Of course, as the models have evolved, the outputs now are "structurally complete," "logically coherent," even convincingly mimicking an author's writing style and tone, referencing their life and experiences.

Everything about it looks like what a first author would write — and yet. There's no *ding* in your head when you read it. No resonance.

Where exactly does human warmth live?

## Dan Koe's Core Argument: AI Is Rewriting What "Meaning" Means

Dan Koe made a point in his podcast that really stuck with me:

> The potential displacement of jobs by AI presents an opportunity to redefine work as a source of meaning.

That's a brilliant reframe. The jobs AI can replace — are those really the things we should be pouring our lives into?

Is "work" about to be redefined?

Whether you're using NotebookLM or Obsidian, AI can only assist your learning and cognition. AI-summarized content passes through your eyes but not your brain. But the stuff you read slowly, take notes on, build double-links for, tag — that actually becomes part of how you think.

Why? Because that "organized" content is missing the most important thing: *my* perspective.

Dan Koe also said:

> As AI commoditizes skills, one's individual taste, perspective, and story become scarce resources that drive value as a creative.

The future of work is shifting from the "creator economy" to the "meaning economy." When AI turns skills into commodities, personal taste, perspective, and story become the scarce resources.

This is so easy to prove right now. AI tools are proliferating wildly, updates coming nonstop. Take video generation: first Kling and Hailuo, now Seedance 2.0 and Tiangong. The tools keep getting more powerful — but how many people are actually using them well, to hit their actual goals?

### A Brief History of Meaning

Dan Koe also traced how meaning has evolved throughout history:

> For most of human history, meaning was given to you by gods, kings, elders, and scriptures.

> During the Industrial Revolution, meaning was found from out there. Science replaced religion as the dominant framework. Meaning shifted to productivity and progress.

> Now, in Act III where we are, meaning has been deconstructed into nothing. Meaning is found nowhere.

> This leads to Act 4, where meaning will be found in here. Meaning has to be generated from within, by you, for you.

One sentence version: *Creatives are the meaning architects of society.*

## Reality Is Validating This Fast

Two weeks ago (February 5, 2026), OpenAI and Anthropic released major new models on the same day — GPT-5.3-Codex and Claude Opus 4.6. Sam Altman also said at the India AI Summit: "By the end of 2028, most of the world's intelligence will exist in data centers."

This is strikingly aligned with Dan Koe's thesis. Dario Amodei also predicted: "In 2026-2027, a single AI model could match the collective intelligence of tens of thousands of top-tier geniuses." When intelligence itself becomes cheap, the uniquely human value is knowing how to give that intelligence meaning.

Just counting the Python scripts sitting on my computer: WeChat sticker generator, CapCut auto-editing, long-form video auto-generation, YouTube Shorts auto-generation, AI news aggregation, various TTS tools, auto web form submission…

The efficiency gains are real. The CapCut automation, for example, can one-click edit hundreds of clips, adding keyframes, dynamic effects, and trimming audio breath pauses for each one. What would take me half a day by hand takes AI 2 seconds.

But a lot of my projects need constant human involvement — semi-automated, you could call them. And those semi-automated projects? Those are the ones that actually succeeded. The video that brought in the most revenue was semi-automated: within the AI workflow, I kept manually adjusting and intervening, refining toward what I thought was the ideal result. And the market validated it.

One video, 80+ million views, 190,000 subscribers:

![](/images/Pasted_image_20260228121947.png)

This made me realize: I was walking in the wrong direction.

I had all these automation pipelines, but mindlessly running them produced content that was just… passable. Every improvement required constant iteration inside the code, which burned energy. The content that succeeded always followed this path: conversational validation → manual assembly → market validation.

"*Build something crappy first.*" "*Get your hands dirty.*" The value of this principle keeps going up.

Dan Koe's "swap test" really clicked for me:

> If you can swap the creator and the creation without it losing value, AI can replace it. If the value is tied to who made it, that's your moat.

I thought about my own wrong turns. Content that was pixel-perfect copies of viral hits got flagged as "reusing others' content" and failed YPP review. But the stuff infused with my perspective, my experiments, my hard-won lessons — that's what went viral.

In the AI era, a creator's moat is probably just *being human*. And that means your values, your feelings, where you came from, your care for others.

## Five Human Attributes AI Can't Copy

Dan Koe identified five attributes AI cannot replicate:

### 1. Perspective
> AI can think about your perspective, but it cannot think from it.

AI can't be a first-generation immigrant. It can't understand what that specific experience actually feels like from the inside.

### 2. Energy Signature
> Two people can write about the same topic and it will feel completely different. AI can mimic the style but cannot mimic care.

### 3. Sensemaking
> AI can process information but it cannot decide what information means. It cannot frame, it cannot prioritize.

Sensemaking requires having something at stake. AI has no stake. Most of the time, we're still the ones controlling the process and determining what matters.

### 4. Trajectory
> You have a past, a present, a future. You have a story arc. AI doesn't have a trajectory.

### 5. Evolving Taste
> If I tell AI to write like me and I read it, I don't want to write about that anymore. My taste instantly changes.

*AI gets stuck in loops. We grow.*


## A Framework Distilled From Practice

Dan Koe's post-AI skill stack resonated with me deeply:

**Agency → Taste → Perspective → Persuasion → Technical Know-how**

### 1. Agency
The ability to act without permission or a prompt.

Personal take: The real growth started when I rejected the "school → job → retire at 65" default path and started doing things I actually cared about.

### 2. Taste
Dan Koe says: "Selection matters more than creation."

Personal take: I've started building my own libraries — character archetypes, script structures, viral elements. This isn't just collecting. It's constant filtering, iteration, building my own aesthetic standards through practice.

### 3. Perspective
Open your mind, reduce dogma, develop the ability to hold complexity, contradiction, nuance.

Personal take: Once I stopped chasing trending topics and focused on what I genuinely care about, the content I made became far more distinctive.

### 4. Persuasion
Getting people to care about what you're making.

Personal take: Content is just step one. Getting it seen, understood, and resonated with — that matters equally.

### 5. Technical Know-how
This is the container, not the core.

Personal take: The way I use Obsidian now is simple. Real thoughts and outputs live there, tagged and cross-linked. When I need to create something, I can retrieve exactly what I need. (Never again am I importing hundreds of thousands of words into a knowledge base under the delusion that it'll help…)

## A New Division of Labor

Chris Paik said it beautifully:

> The elegance of the future is not in man versus machine, but in their division of labor. Silicon sands down the rough edges of necessity so carbon can ascend to meaning. We will abolish baristas and canonize chefs. AI handles the friction, humans handle the narrative.

Especially that line: *Silicon sands down the rough edges of necessity so carbon can ascend to meaning.* As a carbon-based life form, I completely agree.

AI handles friction, humans handle narrative.

The fully automated one-click videos? Flat metrics. The work refined with AI as a collaborator? That's what went viral.

Not because human involvement is automatically higher quality — but because the process involves humans using their own perspective, taste, and story to give the content meaning.

AI makes coding easy — anyone can build a program by just talking to it. But that doesn't mean a jump in efficiency automatically meets real-world needs. Tools are just tools. They amplify the advantages you already have. They're leverage, not a shortcut.

So use AI, yes — but use it to work for you. Don't let it think for you. The moment it starts thinking for you, you'll find yourself one step further from your own goals.

*That's a wrong turn.*
