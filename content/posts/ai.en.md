Have you ever imagined saying "buy me a portable charger" to an AI — and it actually places the order?

Not sending you a recommendation link to click. Not bouncing you to some other app to browse. Actually picking the product, placing the order, and handling payment for you.

Whether or not you've imagined it, it's already starting to happen.

In late February 2026, ByteDance's Doubao app quietly began beta-testing a new feature: **AI-powered shopping**. Users can tell Doubao what they want through voice or text, and it recommends products, inserts product cards into the conversation, and lets you complete payment right inside the app. No jumping to Douyin (TikTok China). No opening Taobao. No comparing prices across platforms.

One sentence. Done.

ByteDance plans to officially launch this feature as early as March 2026. Before this, back in October 2025, Doubao had already rolled out a "product card" feature — when you chatted about anything shopping-related, it would automatically recommend products from Douyin's marketplace. But back then, you still had to tap through to Douyin to actually buy anything.

This time is different. This time it's a closed loop.

## A "Super Gateway" with 145 Million Daily Users Starts Selling Stuff

Let me throw a number at you so you understand why this matters.

According to QuestMobile data, Doubao's user base has surpassed DeepSeek since August 2025, consistently topping the Apple App Store rankings. During the 2026 CCTV Spring Festival Gala, Doubao hit **145 million daily active users**.

145 million. Daily.

For perspective? When Pinduoduo went public, its monthly active users were just over 300 million. Doubao, as an AI assistant, already has nearly 150 million people using it *every single day*.

So ByteDance's logic is crystal clear: I've got a mega traffic pool with over 100 million daily actives. These users chat with Doubao every day, asking questions, getting things done — so why not sell them stuff during the conversation?

Yeah, that logic sounds... very ByteDance.

![](/images/illustration-1.png)

## One-Sentence Ordering: How Does It Actually Work?

Based on what's been leaked from the beta, here's roughly how Doubao's AI shopping flow works:

1. You tell Doubao: "Recommend me a pair of Bluetooth earbuds under 200 yuan"
2. Doubao searches Douyin's e-commerce product database, filtering for merchants and products rated 4.8 and above
3. It inserts product cards into your conversation showing price, ratings, sales volume, etc.
4. You confirm, and complete payment right inside the Doubao app

The entire process requires zero app-switching. No opening Douyin, no third-party redirects.

But here's a detail worth noting — Doubao's shopping experience is currently a "*semi-closed loop*." What does that mean? Payment can indeed happen within Doubao, but the product detail pages, transaction system, and after-sales support all still belong to Douyin's e-commerce infrastructure. If you want to check product specs, return something, or contact customer service, you're going back to Douyin's ecosystem.

So strictly speaking, Doubao is playing the role of a "front-end gateway" — it lowers the barrier to ordering to the absolute minimum (one sentence), but the entire backend of product supply chain, logistics, and customer service is still handled by Douyin's e-commerce platform.

Honestly, that's a pretty smart move. No need to rebuild a transaction system, no payment license compliance headaches. Lightweight entry, get users hooked on the habit first.

## Qwen Was Already Doing This — Doubao Is Playing Catch-Up

Here's the thing though: Doubao isn't the first to do AI shopping.

Alibaba's Tongyi Qwen has already achieved a much more thorough "full closed-loop" AI shopping experience. The Qwen app has fully integrated Taobao, Alipay, Taobao Flash Purchase, Fliggy (travel booking), Amap (navigation), and other services across Alibaba's entire ecosystem. You can say "Book me a flight to Shanghai tomorrow afternoon" in Qwen, and it handles everything — from search and recommendations to ordering and payment — with a built-in "Alipay AI Pay" feature. One-tap payment, zero app-switching required throughout the entire process.

The gap is actually pretty significant.

Qwen's confidence comes from Alibaba's ecosystem — Taobao has massive product data, Alipay has payment infrastructure, Fliggy has travel resources, Amap has mobility services. Stack all of that together and Qwen becomes a "get-anything-done" super assistant. Alibaba's own positioning is quite explicit: they want Qwen to be "the world's first AI assistant capable of completing complex real-life tasks," *leading AI from the 'chat era' into the 'getting things done era.'*

(Okay, that slogan sounds a bit grandiose, but they are actually doing it.)

![](/images/illustration-2.png)

Doubao, on the other hand, with its semi-closed loop approach, is clearly still in exploration mode. While Douyin's e-commerce has plenty of products covering high-frequency categories like baby products, beauty, and home goods, it still can't match Taobao's full-category depth.

That said, Doubao has its own ace: **traffic**. 145 million daily actives is no joke. And ByteDance's content ecosystem (Douyin short videos, livestreams) is inherently tied to consumption — you chat with Doubao about "what sunscreens are trending lately," it drops a product card right there, and that conversion path is way shorter than opening Taobao and searching manually.

So the AI shopping battle lines look roughly like this:

- **Qwen**: Full closed loop, deep ecosystem, can buy anything, but still growing its user base
- **Doubao**: Semi-closed loop, massive traffic, move fast and figure it out along the way
- **Taobao itself**: Not building a standalone AI shopping assistant, but adding AI features within its own app (AI universal search, AI virtual try-on, photo-based price matching) — taking the "add AI to e-commerce" route rather than "add e-commerce to AI"

Three paths, one destination: getting you to spend money using nothing but your voice.

## AI Does Your Shopping — Are You Actually Okay with That?

Alright, enough about features. Let's talk about what actually concerns me.

AI shopping sounds cool, but there are a few issues I think everyone should think through carefully.

**First: privacy.**

For an AI shopping assistant to make purchase decisions for you, it needs to *know* you. It needs your browsing history, purchase records, spending preferences, and even the life details you casually reveal in conversation — whether you have a baby at home, whether you've been trying to lose weight, what your budget roughly looks like.

Add all of that together, and you've got an incredibly detailed "consumer profile" of you.

Kaspersky pointed out in a 2026 retail security report that external AI shopping agents continuously access user behavioral data and aggregate detailed behavioral profiles outside the direct control of users and retail platforms. In other words, you think you're just casually chatting with AI about what to buy, but the system behind it might already understand your spending habits better than you do.

And that's not even getting into image-based product search — you snap a photo asking AI to find you something similar, and that photo might contain your face, your home environment, or even a delivery slip with your name, phone number, and address on it.

If that information gets misused or leaked... well, use your imagination.

![](/images/illustration-3.png)

**Second: impulse buying.**

This is what worries me the most.

Traditional shopping has a natural "cooling-off period" built in — you have to open an app, search, browse, compare prices, add to cart, confirm the order, enter your password. That entire process is annoying, sure, but it gives you plenty of exit ramps to think "actually, never mind."

AI shopping compresses that entire process into one sentence.

"Buy me X" — Done, ordered.

No room for hesitation. No price comparison. No "let me sleep on it" buffer. On top of that, AI will recommend things "you might also need" based on your preferences, leveraging urgency and scarcity ("only 3 left in stock," "flash sale ends in 2 hours") to nudge you toward checkout.

Research shows that AI-driven personalized recommendations significantly boost online shopping conversion rates — sounds great for merchants, but for consumers, it means you're more likely to buy things you didn't actually need.

And then there's returns.

E-commerce return rates generally hover between 12% and 15%, with some categories hitting 20% to 30%. When AI makes ordering frictionlessly easy, return volumes will only go up. And the return experience itself — contacting customer service, filling out forms, waiting for a courier pickup — is not remotely AI-like, not remotely smooth.

> AI is making buying easier and easier, but the return experience hasn't evolved to match. One sentence to order, potentially a week of hassle to return.

**Third: decision quality.**

Is what AI picks for you really the best option?

Don't forget — there's commercial logic behind every AI recommendation. Doubao recommends products from Douyin's marketplace. Qwen recommends products from Taobao. Neither will suggest a cheaper identical item on Pinduoduo, and neither will tell you the same thing costs half as much at your local supermarket.

AI is making the "optimal choice" for you within a walled garden of products — not finding you the best deal across the entire market.

That's not an assistant. That's a sales clerk.

## Will We Actually Use AI Shopping?

Honestly, as someone who works with AI every day, my feelings about AI shopping are... complicated.

Is it convenient? Absolutely. I use my AI assistant (a lobster named PiPiXia) to help me research, organize materials, and write code all the time, and it's genuinely useful. If it could also handle shopping and save me the trouble of opening Taobao to compare three different options, that'd obviously be great.

But I also know that convenience and rationality are often at odds.

Shopping, at its core, is a process that *needs* friction. You need time to figure out whether a product is worth buying. You need to compare different options. You need to distinguish between "I want this" and "I actually need this." That friction looks like an obstacle, but it's actually protection — it protects you from spending money you shouldn't spend on a whim.

What AI shopping does is precisely eliminate that friction.

So here's my personal conclusion: **AI shopping works well for high-frequency, low-stakes purchases** — daily essentials, charging cables, restocking laundry detergent. You don't need to deliberate over these things, and having AI handle them with one tap is genuinely helpful.

But for purchases that deserve serious thought — a laptop, furniture, premium skincare — I'm still going to do my own research, compare prices, and read reviews. AI can help me narrow down the options, but the final decision? I'm not ready to hand that over.

Anyway, Doubao getting into AI shopping doesn't surprise me at all. When an AI assistant hits 145 million daily actives, *not* monetizing that traffic would be the weird move. ByteDance's decision makes complete business sense.

What's really worth paying attention to is this: when AI starts spending your money for you, **are you aware that your purchasing decisions are being shaped by an algorithm?**

That algorithm's objective function isn't "help you save money" or "help you find the best product" — it's "help the platform sell more."

The better the tool, the more we need to stay sharp.
