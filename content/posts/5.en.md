---## You Installed It. Then What?

This is the single most common DM I get.

"Charlotte, I got OpenClaw installed. Now what?"

Honestly? I get it. Because two weeks ago, I was in the exact same spot — followed the deployment guide step by step, watched all the green logs scroll by in the terminal, and thought: *Nailed it.*

Then I opened the chat, sent it a couple of messages, and realized… it was kind of underwhelming? About the same as ChatGPT? Maybe even worse in some ways?

That's not OpenClaw's fault. You just haven't "activated" it yet.

It took me about two weeks of tinkering and stumbling through pitfalls before I figured it out: **Installing OpenClaw is just step one. The real magic comes from these 5 things you do after installation.**

Today I'm laying them all out. These aren't theories — they're hard-won lessons from my own experience.

If you just finished installing OpenClaw, or if you've been running it for a while and think "meh, overrated" — this one might help.

## #1: Switch to the Full Profile

**Skip this, and your lobster is running at 30% power.**

A lot of people start using OpenClaw right after installation without realizing one critical thing: OpenClaw boots into *messaging mode* by default — a lightweight chat-only mode.

What can messaging mode do? Chat. Answer questions. Help you write stuff.

What can't it do? Browse the web. Read and write files. Execute code. Call external tools. In other words, all the things that make OpenClaw actually powerful — doing real work for you, running tasks autonomously, automating workflows — are completely turned off in messaging mode.

It's like buying a new computer and only ever using Safe Mode. It boots, you can poke around, but most software won't run.

**How to switch:**

In your OpenClaw config directory (usually `~/.openclaw/profiles/`), you'll find different profile files. Switch the default from `messaging` to `full`. That's it.

Since the March 2nd, 2026 update, OpenClaw introduced profile isolation — each profile has its own permission scope and toolset. This means you can keep a lightweight messaging profile for casual chat *and* a full profile for real work, running side by side.

For the step-by-step, check the Profile Configuration section at docs.openclaw.ai — it's well documented.

My personal habit? I use full profile for everything. I basically never touch messaging mode — if you're going to raise a lobster, don't treat it like a chatbot.

> After switching to full profile, the most obvious difference is this: you can tell your lobster "search for X" and it actually goes and searches. You can say "save this to a file" and it actually writes the file. None of that works in messaging mode.

## #2: Set Up Your First Chat Channel

**A lobster with no channel is like a phone with no SIM card.**

This is the second most common place beginners get stuck.

After installing OpenClaw, most people interact with it through the terminal or a web interface. It works, but it feels fragmented — you have to deliberately open a page or SSH into your server just to talk to it.

The real sweet spot is when **your lobster lives inside the messaging app you already use every day.**

Like having a coworker in your WeChat. You think of something, fire off a message, and they just handle it. No extra apps, no context switching.

**Currently supported channels:**

- **Telegram** — My top recommendation. Easiest to configure, most complete feature support, lowest message latency. It's what I use.
- **Feishu (Lark)** — Great for Chinese workplace users. If your company runs on Feishu, plug your lobster right in — work communication and AI assistant in one place.
- **DingTalk** — Same idea, for the DingTalk ecosystem.

**How to set it up:**

1. Create a bot on your platform of choice (for Telegram, talk to @BotFather; for Feishu and DingTalk, use the developer console)
2. Grab the bot's token/credentials
3. Add the channel info to your OpenClaw config
4. Restart OpenClaw

Detailed setup guides for each channel are in the Channel Configuration section at docs.openclaw.ai.

I strongly recommend starting with Telegram. Here's why:

First, Telegram's bot ecosystem is the most mature. OpenClaw's feature set is most complete there — inline buttons, file transfers, voice messages, image sending. Pretty much every interaction mode you can think of.

Second, the mobile experience is fantastic. My daily habit now: walking, waiting for the elevator, lying in bed before sleep — I just fire off a message to my lobster assigning a task. It chugs away and replies when it's done.

Third, the setup is genuinely simple. From creating the bot to sending your first message: ten minutes.

> My morning routine now starts with Telegram — not checking other people's messages, but seeing what my lobster got done overnight. It auto-generates daily reports, checks my email, monitors accounts I follow. All stuff I used to spend time on myself.

## #3: Install 3 Essential Skills

**A lobster without Skills is just a smart empty shell.**

I wrote a whole article about this before, but let me hit the highlights again — because "installed it but don't know which Skills to add" consistently ranks in the top three beginner confusions in community feedback.

What are Skills? Simple: they're instruction manuals for your lobster. They tell it how to handle specific types of tasks, what standards to follow, and what to prioritize.

The three must-haves for beginners:

**1. SOUL.md + USER.md + MEMORY.md (The Personalization Trinity)**

These aren't a single Skill — they're three files. But they're the foundation for everything.

- SOUL.md: Tells your lobster who it is (name, personality, working style)
- USER.md: Tells your lobster who you are (your background, goals, preferences)
- MEMORY.md: Gives your lobster long-term memory so it doesn't start from scratch every session

Once these three are in place, your lobster goes from "generic AI" to "your AI." That gap is a quantum leap.

**2. Information Retrieval Skills (agent-reach / platform-fetcher)**

These let your lobster go online. Search the web, scrape pages, read content from Twitter/YouTube/Xiaohongshu (China's Instagram-meets-Pinterest)/Bilibili.

Without these, your lobster is a brilliant person with their eyes closed — smart brain, but can't see what's happening in the world.

I have my lobster scraping competitor content, compiling industry news, and monitoring hundreds of accounts daily. If I did that myself, it'd eat at least an hour every day. Now I spend ten minutes scanning the summaries it prepares.

**3. Writing/Content Skills**

If you create content — social media, blogs, Xiaohongshu posts, video scripts — writing Skills are non-negotiable.

You can encode your writing style, formatting standards, and headline formulas into Skills and feed them to your lobster. That way its output isn't generic AI slop — it's content that carries your personal voice.

**How to install:**

The official Skill marketplace, ClawHub (clawhub.com), has 5,000+ community-contributed Skills. Find what you need and install via command line:

```bash
clawhub install <skill-name>
```

Or write your own. Skills are essentially Markdown files — drop them into your lobster's workspace directory and it'll read them automatically.

> Don't go overboard. Installing dozens of Skills at once will just confuse your lobster. Get the personalization trinity set up first, then add Skills one by one based on your actual needs. I actively use maybe a dozen or so.

## #4: Set Up Cost Alerts

**Skip this, and you might get a bill that makes your heart skip a beat.**

OpenClaw itself is open source and free. But the AI models it calls are not.

Every time your lobster does work, it's calling large language models behind the scenes (Claude, GPT, Gemini, etc.), consuming tokens each time — and tokens cost money.

Most of the time, daily usage costs are modest — maybe a few bucks a month. But if you're not paying attention, costs can spike fast.

I learned this the hard way.

One time I had my lobster running a complex task, and it kept calling the model over and over in the background, going through multiple rounds. I wasn't watching. That evening I checked the API bill — holy cow, one afternoon had burned through nearly $30.

It's not that I can't afford it. It's that you need to *know* where your money is going.

**How to set up cost alerts:**

1. **Set spending limits at your API provider.** Whether you're using Anthropic, OpenAI, or another platform, they all have monthly spending cap settings. Beginners: start with a low cap (say $10/month) and adjust as you get comfortable.

2. **Choose the right model mix.** Not every task needs the most expensive model. Simple tasks can use cheaper models (like Claude Sonnet, GPT-4o mini); save the heavy hitters (Claude Opus, GPT-4o) for complex work. You can set default models in OpenClaw's config.

3. **Build the habit of checking your bills.** Spend two minutes a week glancing at your API usage. Stay informed.

My current setup: Sonnet for daily tasks, Opus when I'm writing long pieces or doing complex analysis. Monthly API cost comes to about $30-40. That's manageable for most people.

But if you set no limits and let your lobster run the most expensive model indefinitely — yeah, the bill will hurt.

> A friend of mine installed OpenClaw and got so excited he had it batch-process a mountain of documents. Used the priciest model. Let it run overnight. Woke up the next morning, checked the bill — three digits. Not three digits in RMB. Three digits in USD. So: set your limits first, *then* let it rip.

## #5: Name Your Lobster + Write SOUL.md

**This is the least "technical" step — but possibly the most important.**

You might be thinking: a name? That counts as "must-do"?

Yes. And I'd argue it has the biggest impact on long-term experience out of all five.

The reason is simple: **Once you name your lobster and write a SOUL.md, it stops being a "tool." It becomes your partner.**

This isn't woo-woo. It's a tangible difference in daily use.

SOUL.md is a file in your lobster's workspace directory that tells it: what's your name, what's your personality, what's your working style, what's your relationship with your human.

Sounds like writing a character backstory? In a way, yeah. But this "backstory" directly shapes how your lobster replies to you — its tone, attitude, and even how it acts autonomously when you haven't given explicit instructions.

My lobster is called PiPiXia — my AI assistant (a lobster named PiPiXia). The name took me half an hour to settle on — don't laugh, naming things is genuinely hard.

PiPiXia's SOUL.md says: it's my technical partner, it should try three solutions before asking me, it reports results first and keeps process details lean, and when it makes a mistake, it documents it so the same error never happens twice.

Sounds simple, right? But those few paragraphs turned it from "an AI that can chat about anything" into "a partner that knows what it should be doing."

**How to write SOUL.md:**

It doesn't need to be complicated. A few core pieces of info will do:

1. **Name** — Call it whatever you want. Serious, goofy, over-the-top — the key is you enjoy saying it and feel something when you do.
2. **Personality** — What vibe do you want? Precise? Funny? Snarky? Gentle? This directly shapes its reply tone.
3. **Working style** — When it hits a problem, should it ask you first or try on its own? Reports: detailed or concise? Any hard no-go's?
4. **Relationship with you** — Is it your assistant, partner, employee, or friend? This framing shapes how much autonomy it takes.

Save it to the root of your workspace directory. Your lobster will read it on next startup.

I've seen all kinds of SOUL.md files in the community — someone set their lobster up as a stern British butler, another wrote theirs as a chatty comedian, someone else configured a silent-but-deadly special ops soldier with insane execution.

There's no right answer. The only standard: **you're comfortable using it, and it gets stuff done.**

> SOUL.md isn't write-once-and-forget. PiPiXia's SOUL.md has been revised at least ten times. Started too simple — lobster was too generic. Then I added too many rules — it got paralyzed. The current version is the result of three months of iteration: concise but precise, controls what needs controlling, lets go of everything else.
