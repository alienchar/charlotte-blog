## The "¥260K in a Few Days" Story — What Actually Happened

Bottom line first: it's probably real. But not the kind of "real" you're imagining.

The person in that 36Kr piece was doing **deployment services**. Translation: tons of people wanted to install OpenClaw on their computers but had no idea how. This person installed it for them. ¥100 to ¥300 per job.

¥100 per install doesn't sound like much, right? But the key is volume. During the peak of the OpenClaw hype wave, the WeChat Index hit 71 million, everyone was saying "I want my own lobster too" — but very few people could actually set it up. Supply-demand imbalance, orders piling up.

¥260K in revenue over a few days wasn't about some proprietary tech. It was about **doing the lowest-barrier thing at exactly the right moment.**

Can you keep making this money? No. Once the one-click installer improves, once tutorials are everywhere, this window closes. Classic timing arbitrage.

But it reveals something bigger: OpenClaw isn't just a product anymore. It's an ecosystem. And the ways to make money around it go far beyond helping people install software.

## 5 Ways to Make Money in the OpenClaw Ecosystem

I've spent a good amount of time observing this ecosystem. The opportunities roughly fall into five categories, ordered from lowest to highest barrier.

### Category 1: Installation & Deployment Services (¥100–1,000/job, Lowest Barrier)

Exactly what I described above — installing OpenClaw for people.

The price range is wide. Basic "get OpenClaw running on your Mac": ¥100. With config optimization, Skill installation, and personalization: ¥500 or even ¥1,000. Enterprise deployment? Even more.

This is the lowest-barrier work in the entire ecosystem. As long as you've installed OpenClaw once, hit the common errors, and can help people remotely troubleshoot — you can take orders. Taobao, Xianyu (China's secondhand marketplace), WeChat groups, Xiaohongshu DMs — demand is everywhere.

But I'll be honest: this window won't last long. As the official installation process gets more foolproof, pure setup demand will drop fast. If you want in, the time is now.

### Category 2: Hardware Sales (Lobster Dens / AI Mini-PCs)

OpenClaw needs to run somewhere — either your computer or a dedicated mini-PC.

This spawned the "lobster den" concept. The most notable is iPollo's Claw PC at $439. A palm-sized box with OpenClaw pre-installed — plug in and go.

Hardware has the fattest margins but also the highest barrier — you need supply chains and after-sales support. Not a lane most individuals can enter.

However, there's an interesting angle: people in China are already building "DIY lobster PCs" — refurbishing old computers with OpenClaw installed and selling them to non-technical buyers. It's basically deployment + hardware bundled together, with better margins than pure installation.

Local governments in Shenzhen, Shanghai, and Wuxi have even rolled out subsidies for AI hardware businesses. If you're in those cities, worth looking into.

### Category 3: Skill Development & Sales

This is the direction I'm most bullish on.

OpenClaw has a skill marketplace called ClawHub. You can develop Skills (think of them as "apps" for your lobster) and sell them or offer paid subscriptions.

Examples: someone built a Skill that auto-monitors competitor accounts on Xiaohongshu. Someone built one that generates daily industry briefings. Someone built one that manages customer WeChat messages. Each solves a specific pain point.

The beauty of this path? **It compounds.** Write one good Skill, sell it many times. Unlike deployment services where you trade time for money, one job at a time.

The barrier? You need some technical understanding. You don't necessarily need to code (many Skills are configured with plain Markdown), but you need to understand how OpenClaw works and what users actually need.

### Category 4: Vertical Applications Built on OpenClaw

According to data from Binance and Phemex, there are currently 129 startups building vertical applications on top of OpenClaw. Their combined monthly revenue is approximately $283,000 — averaging about $2,200 per company per month.

$2,200 a month — not exactly earth-shattering. But most of these are tiny teams that just got started, many with just two or three people. The point is: **they're making money.** Not pitching decks. Not raising pre-A rounds. Actual revenue.

What do these vertical apps look like? AI customer service. Private community automation. Automated financial reporting. Content distribution tools. Each one takes OpenClaw's base capabilities and wraps a vertical industry layer on top.

If you have deep experience in a specific industry and know which processes are the most painful, repetitive, and ripe for automation — this might be your biggest opportunity. You can find technical co-founders. Industry know-how is the real moat.

### Category 5: Training Courses

Every tech wave follows the same playbook: first the installation rush, then the education rush.

OpenClaw is no exception. People are already selling "OpenClaw deployment masterclasses," "Skill development bootcamps," and "AI Agent monetization courses." Prices range from ¥99 to several thousand.

Can you make money here? Yes. Is it sustainable? Depends on your content quality.

Let me be blunt: most training course content is freely available online. What you're paying for isn't the information — it's the curation and the hand-holding. If a course just reads the official docs out loud, it's worth about as much as a ¥9.9 ebook.

But if an instructor has genuine battle scars, can teach you things the docs don't cover, and can help students go from zero to one — that training has real value.

I know you're thinking: "Lots of talk about other people. What about you?"

Expensive? Yeah. Especially that $200/month Claude Max subscription — I wince every time it charges.

But here's context: I once burned through $288 in API fees in just a few hours. That one *really* stung. After that, I got smart — set budget caps on my coding plan, use cheaper models whenever possible. Lessons paid for in blood.

Before OpenClaw, I relied on Claude Code for most of my programming work, and the revenue was coming from those earlier efforts. Looking at OpenClaw alone, I've been using it for less than a month — too early to see direct output.

But if we're counting "efficiency gains converted to time value" — it's already saved me enormous amounts of creative time. Competitor monitoring, research compilation, content organization. I take the hours I save and pour them into strategic thinking and international growth.

So my ledger isn't "spent ¥1,600, made X back." It's "spent ¥1,600, gained several extra hours every day." Those hours are the reason I can push multiple projects forward while maintaining a life.

**That's my reality. Not profitable yet, but not a loss either.**

## What's Real vs. What's a Scam

Every hype wave has two faces. The OpenClaw ecosystem is no different.

**These are probably real opportunities:**
- Deployment services backed by genuine technical skill (not just reading tutorials out loud)
- Skills that solve real pain points
- Vertical apps built on industry know-how
- High-quality content/training based on actual experience

![illustration-4](/images/illustration-4.png)

**These should raise your guard:**

**1. "Uninstall services."** Yes, you read that right. People are selling "help you uninstall OpenClaw" for ¥9.9 to ¥299. Installed it wrong, now paying to remove it. The demand is real (people do mess up their installations), but ¥299 to uninstall software? Think about that.

**2. Token reselling gray markets.** People running API key proxies, selling "cheap tokens," sharing accounts. This isn't just a scam risk — it's a legal one. China's National Internet Emergency Response Center (CNCERT) has already issued security warnings specifically mentioning OpenClaw-related risks, including data leaks and account security threats from third-party token relay services.

**3. "Learn to Make Money with OpenClaw in 3 Days" crash courses.** In three days, you can learn to install it. You cannot learn to make money. Making money requires market understanding, user empathy, and sustained execution. No three-day course gives you that.

**4. Various "Get Rich with Lobster" groups.** They pull you into a group, flood it with daily screenshots of "students earning ¥X0,000/month," then sell you courses or tools. This playbook has been running since the WeChat micro-business era — it just has an AI skin now.

The filter is actually simple: **Is it making money from your growth, or from your anxiety?**

Helping you solve real problems, teaching replicable methods, leaving you feeling the money was well spent — that's a legitimate business.

Exploiting your FOMO, manufacturing urgency, leaving you empty-handed after payment — that's a scam.

## The Realistic Path for Regular People

At this point, you might ask: so what should I actually do?

My advice boils down to six words: **Learn to use it, then monetize.**

Don't start by asking "how can I make money with OpenClaw." If you haven't even figured out what it is, haven't gotten comfortable using it yourself, and you're already thinking about selling courses and services — that's not entrepreneurship. That's following a trend.

**Step 1: Install it yourself. Start using it.**

You don't need to spend a lot. I wrote a piece on the "budget lobster plan" before — you can keep a lobster running for about ¥30 a month. Start by having it solve one real problem for you — even if it's just compiling daily news, managing your calendar, or writing weekly reports.

**Step 2: Find your "unfair advantage."**

Are you in e-commerce? Then you understand e-commerce pain points better than 99% of people. Finance? You know which reports are begging to be automated. Education? You know what teachers and students each need.

OpenClaw is just a tool. The tool itself isn't valuable. **Tool + your industry expertise = something nobody else has.**

**Step 3: Start small. Validate demand.**

Don't quit your job and go all in. Try in your spare time — install OpenClaw for a friend and see the reaction. Build a Skill, put it on ClawHub, see if anyone downloads it. Share your experience on social media, see if anyone asks questions.

If someone pays, the demand is real. If nobody cares, adjust direction.

**Step 4: Then scale.**

After you've validated demand, *then* consider investing more time and resources. By this point, you know what works and what doesn't, so you won't waste money.

Cities like Shenzhen, Shanghai, and Wuxi all have AI startup grants and industry support policies. If you've validated a viable direction, these resources are there for the taking.

## Final Thoughts

Back to the original question: Is anyone really making ¥260K a month?

Yes. But it's the result of a specific time window + specific skills + massive execution. It's not a formula you can simply copy.

Real money exists in the OpenClaw ecosystem — 129 startups generating $283K in monthly revenue prove that. But between "money exists" and "you'll make it" lies a journey called "actually understanding this thing, then finding your place in it."

I spend real money keeping my lobster running every month, and I haven't broken even yet. But I'm not anxious about it, because I know what I'm building — not a quick-flip project, but a system and workflow that can produce value continuously.

So if you want my advice, it's one sentence: **Don't let "¥260K a month" go to your head, and don't give up because you haven't broken even yet.** Get good with the tool first. Figure out your industry advantage. Then take it one step at a time.
