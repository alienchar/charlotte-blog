One Man, One Dog, Two AI Tools: The Cancer Story That Broke the Internet

March 15, 2026· 6min read

My X timeline got flooded these past couple of days with a single tweet.

“Australian programmer, zero biology background, used ChatGPT and AlphaFold to design an mRNA cancer vaccine from scratch and saved his dog.”

Over 70,000 likes. 8 million impressions.

Demis Hassabis personally retweeted it saying “This is just the beginning of digital biology.” Greg Brockman also shared it.

Honestly, when I first saw it, I felt it too. One person, one dog, two AI tools, taking on cancer.

But I have this problem — the more beautiful a story sounds, the more I want to dig into the details.

So today, instead of retelling that viral tweet, I’m going to pull up the original reporting, go through it line by line, and figure out: what’s actually true, what got simplified, and what quietly got amplified in the retelling. What actually happened here?

The Story Itself

Let’s start with what happened. This part is real — and genuinely remarkable.

Paul Conyngham, from Sydney, Australia, adopted a dog named Rosie — a Staffy/Shar Pei mix — from a shelter in 2019. Five years later, Rosie was diagnosed with cancer. Chemo and conventional treatments were tried. Nothing worked.

So Paul decided to do it himself.

He spent AUD $3,000 (roughly USD $2,000) to sequence Rosie’s tumor DNA at the UNSW Ramaciotti Centre for Genomics. With the data in hand, he used ChatGPT to build an analysis plan, AlphaFold to predict mutant protein structures, and matched them to potential drug targets.

His own analogy is pretty clear: “It’s like comparing the original engine of a car to one that’s done 300,000 kilometers — you look for where things are broken.”

Associate Professor Smith at the Genomics Centre later recalled: “Paul was relentless. He called me and said he’d analyzed the data, identified the mutations, predicted the protein structures with AlphaFold, and matched potential targets and drugs. My reaction was — ‘Woah, that’s crazy!’”

After that, Professor Páll Thordarson’s team at the UNSW RNA Institute manufactured a custom mRNA vaccine based on Paul’s blueprint. Injections were administered at the Gatton lab under Professor Allavena.

First injection in December. Second last month. Third this week.

Professor Allavena said: “It’s definitely working. Rosie’s cancer was very advanced, but one tumor has shrunk significantly — roughly halved.”

Up to here: the story is true. And it really is remarkable.

But the Tweet Told a Different Story

Now for the fact-check.

The main source of this viral spread was an account called @IterIntellectus (175k followers), who posted in greentext format and collected nearly 80,000 likes and 8 million views. DeepMind’s AGI policy lead @sebkrier also posted a version that got 8,500 likes.

But compare carefully to the original article (The Australian, journalist Natasha Bita, March 13th), and several key discrepancies stand out.

First: “Zero biology background” — true, but incomplete.

The tweet said Paul had “zero background in biology.” Biologically, yes. But Paul has 17 years of machine learning and data analysis experience. He’s a tech founder, not a random person off the street.

This distinction matters enormously. A person with 17 years of ML experience using ChatGPT to assist with genomic data analysis is completely different from a total layperson starting from scratch. He knows how to ask questions, how to validate AI outputs, how to reason about data pipelines.

The tweet stripped this context out, making the story more dramatic — and less accurate.

Second: “Designed the vaccine from scratch” — he designed the blueprint, not the vaccine itself.

The tweet says “design from scratch.” The original article says “create the blueprint.”

The actual mRNA vaccine was manufactured by Professor Thordarson’s team at the UNSW RNA Institute. Synthesis, preparation, quality control — that’s all professional lab work. Paul’s contribution was the upstream analysis: finding mutations, predicting protein structures, identifying targets.

This isn’t diminishing Paul’s contribution. But “one person used AI to build a vaccine” and “one person used AI for the upstream design analysis, then brought in a top university team to manufacture it” are completely different narratives.

Third: “Outperformed the entire pharmaceutical discovery pipeline” — that’s nowhere in the original.

The tweet includes the line “outperformed the entire pharmaceutical discovery pipeline.” This is the tweet author’s own editorializing. It doesn’t appear anywhere in the original article.

What Paul actually said was: “The red tape was actually harder than the vaccine creation.” He spent two hours per night for three months navigating ethics approvals, filling out 100 pages of paperwork.

That quote was ignored by the retweet chain. Because “I spent three months doing paperwork” doesn’t inspire people.

Fourth: “Probably halved” — the professor’s language is deliberately cautious.

Professor Allavena said “probably halved.” That’s how a scientist talks. But by the time it filtered through the retweet chain, the tone you felt was closer to “cured.”

What did Paul himself say?

“I’m under no illusion that this is a cure, but I do believe this treatment has bought Rosie significantly more time and quality of life.”

He’s clear-eyed: this is not a cure. He’s talking about buying more time and quality of life.

That line is the most important sentence in the entire story — and almost no one in the retweet chain quoted it.

So What Does This Actually Tell Us?

Stepping back, the real conversation worth having isn’t “AI is all-powerful.” And definitely not “AI is about to cure cancer.”

It’s these points:

AI tools are genuinely lowering entry barriers in certain domains. A software engineer with no biological training was able to use ChatGPT and AlphaFold to complete the upstream genomic analysis — mutation identification, protein structure prediction, target matching. That was unimaginable five years ago.

But “lower barrier” doesn’t mean “no barrier.” The vaccine still had to be manufactured by a professional team. The ethics approval still took three months. Paul pulled this off because he simultaneously met several conditions: 17 years of technical expertise, the financial capacity (AUD $3,000 for sequencing), knowing who to collaborate with, and — honestly — extraordinary execution and persistence.

The retweet chain always simplifies the story. “One person used AI to save their dog” is a hundred times more shareable than “a software engineer with 17 years of ML experience used AI for the upstream analysis of vaccine design, then got a university professor’s team to manufacture it, one tumor shrank by roughly half, and he says this isn’t a cure.” But the first is a myth; the second is a real, complex, still-remarkable story.

I prefer the second version.

Because it tells you: AI isn’t a magic wand, but if you have enough technical foundation, enough execution ability, and enough luck — it can be a genuinely useful tool.

Paul is already working on a second vaccine, targeting Rosie’s remaining tumors.

Rosie is still alive.

That’s enough.
