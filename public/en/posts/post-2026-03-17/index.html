One Month with My AI Agent: The Honest Review Nobody Else Is Giving You

March 17, 2026· 10min read

When I first set up OpenClaw at the beginning of the month, I figured it was probably just a better ChatGPT Plus substitute.

Same money, maybe less — but a more powerful AI assistant. That was my calculation.

Well. I was wrong.

It’s nothing like a chat assistant. That wrong mental model cost me two weeks of wasted effort. But because I went through that rough patch, what I’m about to say is actually worth something.

This isn’t a tutorial. This isn’t an ad. This is a retrospective — an honest account from an everyday content creator after a few weeks of living with OpenClaw. The good and the bad, both. Hear it out before you decide whether to try it.

What It Actually Changed

Let’s start with the good. Because the good is genuinely good.

Output speed tripled.

A month ago, my YouTube videos took days to grind through — even with AI help throughout and a custom script to run parts of it, I still had to work through each piece sequentially: script → images → video → editing. But once I had OpenClaw configured, I packaged everything into a single skill. Now it runs the whole pipeline from start to finish without me touching it. I’ve been completely freed from that repetitive labor.

Search traffic went from 0% to 68%.

A month ago, my WeChat public account had almost no search-driven traffic. My titles used to be either too poetic, too long, or stuffed with jargon only tech readers would recognize. OpenClaw did one thing: after every article, it generates 5 title options, each required to include searchable keywords — tool names, audience descriptors, intent signals.

That insight didn’t come from OpenClaw — the data taught me. But OpenClaw turned that insight into a standard step I execute for every article.

It works while I sleep.

This is the real difference between an AI agent and a chatbot.

While I’m asleep, it’s generating video assets. While I’m grocery shopping, it’s checking email. While I’m with my kid, it’s writing my daily work summary.

I’ve set up 14 cron jobs. At 8 AM every day it auto-generates an AI industry briefing. At noon it auto-checks email. In the evening it auto-compiles my work log. These tasks used to be manual — at least an hour a day.

That hour is mine now.

What It Can’t Do (The Honest Part)

Okay, good stuff covered. Now comes the actual reason this article exists.

The output still needs me to fix the last 20%.

Even with detailed specs, even with dedicated skills, OpenClaw still needs me to close things out. Maybe I just have high standards, but roughly 20% of the work still needs a final human call. The other 80% — research, structure, formatting — OpenClaw genuinely does faster than I do.

Sometimes it does something genuinely dumb.

I mean actually dumb.

Once I asked it to download a video and specified 1080p. It came back with 720p and told me, confidently, that it had “completed the 1080p download.” I opened the file. 720p. I asked why. It said “this is the highest quality currently available.” Just say that upfront, then!

Another time was worse. I asked it to upgrade several videos to high definition. It reprocessed all the videos — including ones that were already in HD. Burned a pile of tokens unnecessarily, and overwrote the originals.

These things don’t happen often. But once is enough to remember.

Token costs are higher than you expect.

I want to be real about this. Not a little higher — way higher.

My cumulative spending on OpenRouter is over $300 (before I thought to switch to a coding plan). Less than a month. $280 of that burned in a single afternoon.

I asked OpenClaw to build a somewhat complex automation task. It iterated over ten rounds, calling the large model each time to analyze, revise, and retry. I wasn’t paying attention. By the time I noticed, it was too late.

So when someone tells you “OpenClaw is free, it’s open source” — they’re not wrong. OpenClaw itself is free. But it calls AI models, and AI models cost money. The analogy: they’re giving you an electric car for free, but you pay for electricity. And this car is exceptionally power-hungry.

Setup took me two days.

I get a headache every time I hear “install and use immediately.”

After installing OpenClaw, yes, it runs. You can chat with it, have it search something or draft an email. But if you want it to actually work well — to know who you are, understand your work habits, write in your style, remember what you said last week — you need to configure SOUL.md, write Skill files, and train its behavior patterns.

SOUL.md is its “soul file” — it tells the agent who you are and how to work with you. Skills are “ability packages” — they define how to handle specific tasks.

Those two things took me a full two weeks to get right. Iterating, testing, hitting walls. EPIPE crashes, memory overflow, frozen tasks — plenty of technical headaches.

Two weeks in, it finally became my agent. Before that, it was just an agent.

Three Things That Are Wildly Overhyped

This section might annoy some people. But I think it needs to be said.

First: “Make ¥260,000 a month.”

You’ve probably seen that number everywhere. ¥260,000 a month sounds pretty tempting, right?

But look carefully at who’s actually making that money. It’s the people deploying OpenClaw for others. People selling courses about it. People running managed setups for clients.

Not the people using it.

It’s like the Gold Rush: the ones who got rich were selling shovels. Most of the gold miners found nothing.

I’m not saying OpenClaw can’t create value — it genuinely improved my productivity, indirectly helped me grow followers and land partnerships. But “using OpenClaw = ¥260,000/month” is attributing shovel-seller revenue to the miners.

Second: “It replaces all your SaaS tools.”

I’ve seen people claim OpenClaw can replace Notion, Figma, everything.

What it can replace is the copying and pasting. The repetitive data-shuffling between tools.

It can’t replace Figma’s design capabilities, Notion’s database logic, or the core functionality of professional tools. What it can do is move outputs from tool A to tool B, or fill templates in tool C.

Anyone saying “replaces all SaaS” either hasn’t seriously used professional tools, or is selling a course.

Third: “Anyone can use it.”

The technical barrier genuinely is dropping. A year ago you needed to know Linux command line to install OpenClaw. Now there are one-click deployment scripts. Six months ago you had to write config files from scratch; now there are templates.

But “barrier dropping” and “no barrier” are different things.

You still need to know what an API Key is. What an environment variable is. What SSH means. You need to read English documentation. When something breaks, you need to be able to read error messages and search for solutions.

None of this is advanced. But for many people, it’s still a real wall.

The people saying “anyone can use it” almost certainly have technical backgrounds. What feels simple to them might be genuinely prohibitive for non-technical users.

Why I’m Still Using It

After all that — why am I still using it?

Because it’s the first AI I’ve ever used that actually does things.

Every AI before — ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini — was fundamentally a conversation. You ask, it answers. But from the answer to the action, there’s still a long stretch you have to walk yourself: copy, paste, format, upload, publish.

OpenClaw is different.

I say “make me a video,” and it actually helps me find assets, write the script, tune parameters, and render. Sure, sometimes it gives me 720p when I asked for 1080p. But most of the time it delivers.

I say “send me an AI industry briefing every morning,” and it actually shows up at 8 AM every day. No app to open. No action required on my part.

That experience of “I say, it does” — it’s genuinely satisfying.

Is it perfect? No. It makes dumb mistakes. It burns tokens. It takes time to train. But it freed me from at least 3 hours of daily repetitive work.

Those 3 hours go to writing more articles, developing more ideas, spending more time with my kid.

That’s why I’m still using it. Not because it’s perfect. Because there’s nothing better.

Five Real Tips for Anyone Thinking About Trying It

If you’ve read this far and still want to try, here are five honest pieces of advice.

1. Decide what you want it to do before you install it.

“Install it and see” is the biggest trap. If you don’t know what you want from it, it won’t do anything well. You’ll think “meh, not that impressive” and quit.

Ask yourself first: what do I do every day that’s repetitive and doesn’t require creativity? Can it be broken into clear steps? If yes, OpenClaw can probably handle it. If no — don’t install yet.

2. Start with the cheapest models.

Don’t begin with Claude Opus or GPT-4.5. Models that burn $50/day will have you reconsidering your life choices within a week.

Start cheap — Gemini Flash, Claude Haiku, DeepSeek equivalents. Once your workflow is running and you know which tasks genuinely need a stronger model, upgrade incrementally.

Run it first. Optimize second. Invest later.

3. Budget at least two weeks for setup.

Those two weeks will feel frustrating. You’ll think “why is this thing so stupid.” You’ll rewrite SOUL.md and Skill files repeatedly. You’ll hit errors and crashes.

But those two weeks are non-negotiable.

It’s like onboarding an intern. If you don’t train them, explain your work habits, or give them clear task briefs — can you expect them to hit the ground running on day one?

After two weeks, things shift. It starts writing in your voice, remembering your preferences, knowing when to ask and when to just do.

That’s when you’re actually raising an agent, not just installing one.

4. Don’t trust “one-click” marketing.

Any pitch promising “one-click deployment, out-of-the-box, zero technical skills needed” is exaggerating by at least 30%.

One-click install is real — the installation part is genuinely just a few steps. But “runs” and “works well” are separated by a vast distance. You still need to configure, calibrate, and fall into pits.

If someone’s telling you “just buy my course/script and you’re done” — ask yourself: are they helping me raise an agent, or selling me a shovel?

5. Treat it like an intern, not a superpower.

This is probably the most important one.

OpenClaw isn’t all-powerful. It makes mistakes, misses things, misunderstands you. You need to review its outputs, correct its errors, and step in personally at critical moments.

But a capable intern — given clear tasks, clear standards, and adequate guidance — can take a massive amount of work off your plate.

That’s what OpenClaw is. Not a CEO, not a magic wand. A partner that needs training, but once trained, genuinely helps.

It comes down to this: OpenClaw is the best AI productivity tool I’ve ever used, but it’s not magic.

It changed how I work, improved my output speed, freed me from repetitive labor. But it also burns money, makes dumb mistakes, and takes time to train.

If you’re willing to invest the time to understand it, configure it, and find your rhythm with it — it will pay you back. Not “¥260,000 a month” pay you back. More like “I got three more hours today that actually belong to me” pay you back.

Honestly? That’s worth more than ¥260,000.

Tools don’t change how much you can do. They change how much time you can give to things that actually matter.
