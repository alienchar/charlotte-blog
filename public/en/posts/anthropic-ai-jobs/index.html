Anthropic Studied It Themselves: Will AI Take Your Job?

March 7, 2026· 7min read

On March 5, 2026, Anthropic — the company behind Claude — published a research report.

The title translates roughly to: The Impact of AI on Labor Markets: A New Measure and Early Evidence. Original link: https://www.anthropic.com/research/labor-market-impacts

Yeah. The people who build AI went and studied whether AI will make people unemployed.

Honestly, everyone’s a little nervous these days. On one hand you’re constantly seeing “XYZ model just dropped another update!” and on the other hand you’re thinking: where does that leave the rest of us?

I read the whole paper. The conclusion isn’t as terrifying as the clickbait headlines — it’s definitely not one of those “AI will destroy XX million jobs” pieces. But I don’t know how to describe it other than… a quiet chill. The report is almost unsettlingly calm. Every claim is backed by statistics. And that’s what makes it feel so real.

A New Metric Called “Observed Exposure”

Over the past two years, there’s been a flood of research on “which jobs will AI replace.” Most of it is theoretical: list out all the tasks in a given occupation, then have experts judge whether AI could theoretically do them. Which means, at the end of the day, it’s still humans deciding. Not exactly rigorous.

Anthropic said: this isn’t good enough.

Being theoretically capable of something and actually doing it are two very different things.

Their approach: pull real Claude usage data and look at what people are actually using AI for. Not surveys, not expert panels — statistical analysis of millions of real conversations.

So they created a new metric: observed exposure. Basically, what percentage of tasks in your job are not just theoretically doable by AI, but are already being done by AI — in an automated way, in actual work contexts?

Four conditions, stacked on top of each other.

This filters out all the hot air. It can’t get more real than that.

94% in Theory, 33% in Practice

The most striking chart: for computer and math occupations, LLMs can theoretically cover 94% of tasks.

In actual practice? 33%.

For office and admin jobs, the theoretical coverage is 90%. In reality, only a small fraction.

(The blue bars show theoretical AI coverage; the red bars show actual current usage.)

💡 🎯 Key Insight

AI’s capabilities are way ahead of adoption. It’s not that it can’t do the work — it’s regulations, internal processes, human review requirements, system integration. These “non-technical factors” are the brakes.

But most of these brakes are temporary.

That word “temporary” is what should make you uneasy.

It means the sense of security many people have right now might just be a window of time.

The Top 10 Most At-Risk Jobs — Are You on the List?

Anthropic listed the ten occupations with the highest observed exposure (most likely to be affected by AI). Number one? Computer programmers — 75% of their tasks are already being done by Claude.

Second: Customer service representatives.

Third: Data entry workers.

After that: financial analysts, market research specialists, medical records technicians…

The safest jobs? Cooks, motorcycle mechanics, bartenders, dishwashers, lifeguards.

💡 💡 Counterintuitive Finding

Completely opposite to what most people assume. It’s not blue-collar workers being replaced, it’s white-collar workers. It’s not low-income workers at risk — it’s high earners.

The most-affected group earns 47% more on average than the safe group. And they’re nearly four times more likely to have a graduate degree.

I read that last statistic three times. Especially: nearly four times more likely to have a graduate degree.

But it shatters a deeply entrenched narrative: “AI will take the bottom rung jobs.”

Wrong. AI is coming for the work done in air-conditioned offices, staring at computers, processing information. (That used to be my job — writing emails, making PowerPoints, attending meetings, building spreadsheets. I laughed at this, and also felt like: yeah, fair enough.)

Because at its core, AI is an information processing machine. The more “information-based” your job is, the better it can do it.

This table is worth a close look. Another gut punch.

The groups most likely to be affected:

  • High education + high salary
  • Female + married + Asian/White
  • Long working hours + non-union

The profile most at risk: A female professional with at least a college degree, higher hourly wages, and not in a union.

Ouch.

No Mass Layoffs — But Young People Can’t Get In

There’s a subtle section in the report worth flagging.

Anthropic used U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics data to run a difference-in-differences analysis. Their finding:

From ChatGPT’s launch in 2022 to now, unemployment rates in the most exposed occupations have not significantly increased.

Sounds like good news, right?

Wait.

They also looked at another dimension: job entry rates for 22-25 year olds.

In high-exposure occupations, young people’s chances of landing new jobs dropped by 14%.

For workers over 25? No significant change.

It’s not that people are getting laid off. It’s that companies stopped hiring.

This means AI’s impact on the job market won’t look like the 2008 financial crisis — mass layoffs overnight, headlines everywhere.

It’s quieter: the job still exists, but the next open position isn’t going to a new hire. Companies hire 10 junior programmers this year, 5 next year, 2 the year after. Nobody gets “fired,” but the entrance for an entire generation is narrowing.

You see news saying: fresh graduates are having a harder and harder time finding jobs. Nothing looks dramatically different, but the résumés just aren’t getting callbacks.

What the Report Doesn’t Say Out Loud

Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei has said publicly that AI could replace up to half of all entry-level white-collar jobs within 1-5 years.

Microsoft AI chief Mustafa Suleyman has said similar things.

Interestingly, the research report itself is extremely restrained in its language. It uses phrases like “suggestive evidence.” It says effects are “indistinguishable from zero.” It keeps emphasizing this is “early evidence.”

I think they’re saying: we know this will make you anxious, but please, don’t panic yet…

Why would an AI company publish a report like this?

My read: not to defend themselves, but to establish a baseline.

They’re saying: look, we’ve drawn a line in the sand. We can now track this. When the line starts moving, we — and society — will be able to see it.

This is infinitely more useful than “AI will eliminate XX billion jobs” panic headlines. Panic doesn’t solve problems. Tracking does.

What Does This Have to Do With Me?

If you’re a mid-to-senior-level white-collar worker — programmer, analyst, marketer, project manager — you’re probably not going to lose your job in the short term. Remember that chart: the blue area (theoretical impact) is huge, the red area (actual current impact) is small. It takes time for the red to spread.

This report gives us data to back that up.

But if you’re still comforting yourself with “AI is just a tool, it can’t replace real people,” I’d suggest looking at that chart again: 94% theoretical coverage, 33% actual usage.

That 61% gap isn’t “AI can’t do it” — it’s “it just hasn’t gotten there yet.”

If you’re 22-25 and just starting out in your career, this report is even more relevant to you. Your competition isn’t just your peers anymore. It’s something that doesn’t sleep, doesn’t take sick days, and doesn’t need health insurance.

We don’t need to panic. But we do need to honestly assess: of everything we do every day, how much of it could AI already do — and just hasn’t gotten around to yet?

I’m someone who left a Fortune 500 company to go full-time on AI-powered work. Now everything I do every day involves AI collaboration. I know very clearly: the reason I still have value isn’t that AI can’t do what I do — it’s that I know what’s worth doing, when to let AI do it, and when to do it myself.

That judgment? AI doesn’t have it yet.

But “yet” — like “temporary” — doesn’t mean forever.

The real moat isn’t what you can do. It’s knowing what’s worth doing.
