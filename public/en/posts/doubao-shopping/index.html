Doubao Can Now Buy Things for You. Here's Why You Should Pause Before Using It.

March 17, 2026· 9min read

Have you ever imagined telling an AI “buy me a power bank” — and it actually places the order?

Not recommending a link for you to click. Not redirecting you to an app to browse. Just: it picks one, orders it, pays for it.

Whether you imagined it or not, it’s starting to happen.

In late February 2026, ByteDance’s Doubao app quietly started beta testing a new feature: AI-powered shopping. Users can express a shopping need directly in Doubao via voice or text, and Doubao will recommend products, insert product cards, and let you complete payment inside the app. No jumping to Douyin. No opening Taobao. No comparison shopping.

One sentence. Done.

ByteDance plans to officially launch this feature as early as March 2026. Before that, in October 2025, Doubao already launched a “product card” feature — during chat conversations on consumer topics, it would automatically recommend Douyin Mall product links. But back then you still had to tap through to Douyin to buy anything.

This time it’s different. This time it’s fully closed-loop.

The “Super Gateway” with 145 Million Daily Users Starts Selling Things

Let me put a number up first, so we understand why this matters.

According to QuestMobile data, Doubao’s user base surpassed DeepSeek’s from August 2025 onward, consistently topping the Apple App Store charts. During the 2026 Spring Festival Gala broadcast, Doubao’s daily active users reached 145 million.

145 million.

For context: when Pinduoduo went public, its monthly actives were just over 300 million. Doubao, as an AI assistant, already has daily actives approaching 150 million.

So ByteDance’s logic is crystal clear: I have a super-platform with over 100 million daily users, all of them chatting with Doubao every day, asking questions, getting things done — so why not let them shop while they’re at it?

That logic is… very ByteDance.

How Does One-Sentence Shopping Actually Work?

Based on available beta information, Doubao’s AI shopping flow looks roughly like this:

  1. You tell Doubao “recommend a Bluetooth headset under ¥200”
  2. Doubao filters through its Douyin e-commerce catalog for merchants and products rated 4.8 or above
  3. It inserts product cards into the conversation, including price, rating, and sales volume
  4. You confirm, and complete payment directly inside the Doubao app

The entire process doesn’t require opening Douyin or redirecting to any third-party page.

But there’s a detail worth noting — Doubao’s current shopping experience is what you’d call a “semi-closed loop.” Meaning: yes, you can complete payment inside Doubao. But the product detail page, transaction system, and after-sales process all still belong to Douyin’s e-commerce infrastructure. To read full product details, initiate a return, or contact customer service, you’re ultimately back in Douyin’s system.

So strictly speaking, Doubao is currently serving as the “front-end gateway” — it drops the barrier to placing orders as low as possible (one sentence), but the supply chain, logistics, and after-sales behind it are all still Douyin e-commerce’s territory.

That’s actually a smart approach. No need to rebuild a transaction system, no payment licensing compliance from scratch — light-touch entry, build user habits first.

Tongyi Qianwen Got There Earlier, and Went Further

Worth noting: when it comes to AI shopping, Doubao isn’t actually the first mover.

Alibaba’s Tongyi Qianwen app already achieved a more complete “fully closed-loop” AI shopping experience. Qianwen is fully integrated with Taobao, Alipay, Taobao Flash Delivery, Fliggy, and AutoNavi across the Alibaba ecosystem. Say to Qianwen “book me a flight to Shanghai tomorrow afternoon,” and it can take you from search through recommendation to checkout and payment in one flow — with a built-in “Alipay AI Pay” feature, one-tap payment, without leaving the app at any point.

The gap is actually quite visible.

Qianwen’s advantage comes from Alibaba’s ecosystem — Taobao’s massive product catalog, Alipay’s payment infrastructure, Fliggy’s travel inventory, AutoNavi’s mobility services. Stack all of that together and Qianwen becomes a “get anything done” super-assistant. Alibaba’s own positioning is explicit: they want Qianwen to become “the world’s first AI assistant capable of completing genuinely complex real-life tasks,” leading AI from the “chat era” into the “get-things-done era.”

(Honestly, that slogan sounds big — but they’re actually building toward it.)

Doubao’s “semi-closed loop,” by contrast, signals that it’s still in the exploration phase. Douyin’s e-commerce catalog isn’t small — strong coverage in parenting, beauty, and home categories — but it’s a different depth from Taobao’s full-category breadth.

That said, Doubao has its own edge: traffic. 145 million daily actives isn’t a joke. And ByteDance’s content ecosystem (Douyin short video, livestreaming) is naturally tied to consumption — you finish chatting with Doubao about “what’s the trending sunscreen right now,” and it immediately drops a product card. That conversion path is considerably shorter than opening Taobao and searching from scratch.

So the AI shopping landscape currently looks like this:

  • Qianwen: fully closed-loop, deep ecosystem, buy anything, but still growing its user base
  • Doubao: semi-closed loop, massive traffic, ship it and iterate
  • Taobao itself: not building a standalone AI shopping assistant — instead adding AI features inside its own app (AI smart search, AI try-on, photo search for lower prices), going “AI inside e-commerce” rather than “e-commerce inside AI”

Three paths. One destination: let you spend money with your voice.

AI Does Your Shopping. Are You Okay With That?

Alright, enough about the features. Let me talk about what I actually find worth thinking carefully about.

AI shopping sounds great. But there are a few questions I think everyone should sit with.

First: privacy.

For an AI shopping assistant to help you make purchase decisions, it needs to know you. It needs your browsing history, purchase records, spending preferences — and even the personal details you let slip in conversation: do you have a baby at home, are you trying to lose weight, what’s roughly your budget?

Put that data together and you have an extraordinarily detailed “consumer profile of you.”

Kaspersky, in a 2026 retail security report, noted: external AI shopping agents continuously access user behavioral data and aggregate detailed behavioral profiles outside the direct control of users and retail platforms. In other words: you think you’re just chatting about what you want to buy. The system behind the AI may already understand your spending habits better than you do.

And that’s before you get to image-based product search — you take a photo to ask the AI to find something similar, and that photo might contain your facial features, your home environment, and the name, phone number, and address on a delivery label.

Once that information is misused or leaked, the consequences… you can imagine.

Second: impulse buying.

This is my biggest concern.

Traditional shopping has a natural “cooling-off period” built in — you have to open an app, search, browse, compare, add to cart, confirm the order, enter your password. That whole process is tedious, but it gives you many chances to bail: “actually, never mind.”

AI shopping compresses that entire process to one sentence.

“Buy me an X” — got it, ordered.

No space to hesitate. No comparison process. No “let me think about it” buffer. And the AI will recommend “things you might also need” based on your profile, using urgency and scarcity (“only 3 left in stock,” “limited-time offer ends in 2 hours”) to push you toward checkout.

Research data shows that AI-driven personalized recommendations significantly increase conversion rates in online shopping — which sounds good for merchants, but for consumers means you’re more likely to buy things you shouldn’t have.

Then come the returns.

E-commerce return rates are generally 12–15%, sometimes reaching 20–30% in certain categories. When AI makes placing orders effortless, the return volume will only increase. And the return experience itself — contacting customer service, filling out forms, waiting for courier pickup — is not remotely AI-smooth. Not even close.

AI makes buying easier and easier. But the return experience hasn’t evolved at the same pace. One sentence to buy; potentially a week to send back.

Third: decision quality.

Is what the AI picks actually the best option for you?

Don’t forget — AI product recommendations are backed by commercial logic. Doubao recommends from Douyin’s e-commerce catalog; Qianwen recommends from Taobao. They won’t suggest the same item that might be cheaper on Pinduoduo, and they won’t tell you the offline supermarket has it at half price.

The AI is making the “best choice” within a closed product pool — not finding you the genuinely best option across the whole market.

That’s not an assistant. That’s a sales rep.

Will We Actually Shop This Way?

Honestly, as someone who works with AI every day, my feelings about AI shopping are genuinely mixed.

Is it convenient? Absolutely. I use OpenClaw to research topics, organize materials, and write code, and it works well. If it could also handle shopping and save me the time of opening Taobao and comparison-shopping — that sounds great on paper.

But I also know that convenience and rational decision-making are often in tension.

Shopping, fundamentally, is a process that needs friction. You need time to understand whether something is worth buying. You need to compare different options. You need to judge the gap between “want” and “need.” That friction looks like an obstacle, but it’s actually protection — it keeps you from spending money you shouldn’t have spent on a passing impulse.

What AI shopping does is precisely eliminate that friction.

So my personal conclusion: AI shopping makes sense for high-frequency, low-stakes purchases — daily consumables, charging cables, restocking detergent. You don’t need to deliberate on those; let the AI one-click handle it.

But for purchases that require careful consideration — a laptop, furniture, expensive skincare — I’ll still do my own research, comparison, and review-reading. AI can help me narrow down the options, but the final call? I’m not ready to hand that over.

Anyway, Doubao launching AI shopping doesn’t surprise me. When an AI assistant has 145 million daily users, not monetizing that traffic would be the anomaly. The business logic is airtight.

What’s actually worth watching is: when AI starts spending your money for you, do you realize your consumption decisions are being shaped by an algorithm?

That algorithm’s objective function isn’t “help you save money” or “help you buy the best thing.” It’s “help the platform sell more.”

The better the tool, the clearer we need to be about what we’re doing.
